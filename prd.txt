## 0. Executive Summary — What Changed

Version 1.0 proved the concept. We got top 6 out of 1000 teams on the strength of one idea: **HMIs should be honest about what they don't know.** The confidence scoring engine, the mass-balance cross-check, the shift handover brief — these were right. The judges understood the insight immediately.

Version 2.0 is not more of the same. It is a transformation of ConfidenceOS from a **single-plant monitoring dashboard** into a **full industrial intelligence platform** that judges can walk up to, use without instruction, and immediately understand is production-ready.

The five things V2 adds that V1 could not do:

1. **Predict failures before they happen** — not just flag what is wrong now, but forecast what will fail and when, with a confidence interval.
2. **Let anyone ask the system anything in plain English** — a natural language interface so judges who have never seen an HMI can query the plant like a search engine.

The core thesis does not change:

> **The HMI's job is not just to show data. Its job is to show data AND how much to trust that data.**

V2 extends that thesis from the sensor level to the enterprise level. Every reading earns its trust. Every plant earns its health score. Every operator earns their shift sign-off.

## 1. The Problem — Carried Forward and Extended

### 1.1 The Three Disasters (Unchanged)

All three root incidents from V1 remain the foundation. Nothing in V2 weakens or replaces this grounding — it deepens it.

**BP Texas City Refinery — March 23, 2005**
15 fatalities. 180 injuries. $1.5 billion in damages.
Root HMI failure: sensor confidence blindness, mass-balance invisibility, silent shift handover.

**Three Mile Island — March 28, 1979**
Near-meltdown. $2.4 billion in cleanup.
Root HMI failure: command-state decoupling — the valve indicator showed what was commanded, not what was physically true.

**Esso Longford Gas Plant — September 25, 1998**
2 fatalities. $1.3 billion in damages.
Root HMI failure: no physical plausibility envelope — the temperature reading was correct but catastrophically outside the safe operating range with no contextual warning.

### 1.2 The Extended Problem — What V1 Didn't Solve

V1 solved the single-operator, single-plant, real-time problem. V2 addresses the gaps that remain.

**Gap 1 — Reactive, not predictive.**
V1 flags a sensor when its confidence has already degraded. A sensor at 12% confidence is already a problem that has been developing for days. The ideal system identifies the trajectory of degradation before the sensor reaches dangerous territory. If calibration score decays at a known rate, the system should say: "LT-5100 will cross the LOW threshold in approximately 18 hours at current drift rate." That is the difference between reactive monitoring and predictive maintenance.

**Gap 2 — Single plant, single operator.**
Industrial operations are not single-plant operations. ABB serves clients with dozens of facilities across multiple geographies. An HMI that only shows one plant to one operator is a tool. An HMI that shows a fleet of plants to a control centre manager, prioritized by risk score, is a platform. V1 was a tool. V2 is a platform.

**Gap 3 — No institutional memory.**
When an incident happens, the ability to reconstruct exactly what every sensor showed at every minute is critical — for regulatory compliance, for liability, for learning. V1 stores sensor history but provides no forensic replay interface. V2 adds a full incident reconstruction mode that allows any historical window to be replayed at any speed.

**Gap 4 — Compliance is manual and expensive.**
OSHA, IEC 61511, and ISA-18.2 require documented evidence of alarm management practices, sensor calibration records, and shift handover logs. Currently, operators compile these manually at enormous cost. V2 auto-generates audit-ready compliance reports from the data ConfidenceOS already holds, creating immediate commercial value for any plant that adopts the system.

**Gap 5 — The system is not queryable.**
An operator standing in front of a dashboard full of numbers must know where to look. A better system answers direct questions. "Why is the pressure rising?" "Which sensor has degraded the most this week?" "Is it safe to increase load on vessel 3?" These questions should be answerable in natural language. V2 adds an AI query interface that lets anyone talk to the plant.

### 4.1 Module 7 — Predictive Failure Engine

**Owner:** Backend engineer
**Language:** Python 3.11 + scikit-learn
**Estimated effort:** 4 days
**Dependencies:** V1 confidence scoring engine, SQLite sensor history

#### What It Does

The confidence scoring engine in V1 tells you where a sensor is **right now**. The Predictive Failure Engine tells you where it will be — and when it will cross each critical threshold.

For every sensor, the engine maintains a rolling time-series of confidence scores (stored in SQLite by V1). It fits a degradation model to this series and extrapolates forward to produce:

- **Time-to-LOW forecast:** estimated hours until confidence drops below 50%
- **Time-to-CRITICAL forecast:** estimated hours until confidence drops below 20%
- **Confidence interval:** expressed as a range (e.g., "6–9 hours") based on model fit quality
- **Primary degradation driver:** which sub-score is declining fastest and why
- **Recommended action:** plain-English maintenance instruction with urgency level

#### Degradation Model

The engine uses a three-stage model selection process per sensor:

```
1. Fit linear decay model (calibration age, stable drift)
   → Use if R² > 0.85 and residuals are small

2. Fit exponential decay model (accelerating degradation)
   → Use if readings show accelerating decline pattern

3. Fit step-change detection model (sudden drops)
   → Use if recent history shows abrupt changes

Output: predicted_confidence(t) = model(current_state, t)
        where t is hours from now
```

The model is re-fit every 15 minutes against the most recent 24 hours of confidence history stored in SQLite.

#### UI Output — Predictive Failure Card

Each sensor in the operator dashboard gains a small expandable prediction card:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ LT-5100 — Level Transmitter                     │
│ Current Confidence: 64% MEDIUM                  │
│                                                 │
│ ⚠ Predicted: CRITICAL in ~6 hours               │
│   Range: 5.5–7.2 hours (model fit: good)        │
│   Driver: calibration score decaying at 1.2%/hr │
│                                                 │
│ Action: Schedule calibration within 4 hours.    │
│         Do not use for startup decisions after  │
│         16:00 without manual verification.      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### 4.2 Module 8 — Natural Language Plant Query Interface

**Owner:** AI / Integration lead
**Language:** Python + Claude API + React
**Estimated effort:** 3 days
**Dependencies:** All V1 data sources, SQLite history

#### What It Does

A conversational interface embedded in the right panel of the operator dashboard. Any user — operator, engineer, manager, or judge unfamiliar with HMIs — can type or speak a question and receive a grounded, cited answer in plain English.

The system does not hallucinate. Every answer is grounded in live sensor data and historical records from SQLite. The LLM is instructed to cite the specific sensor, timestamp, and value that supports each claim.

#### Query Types Supported

**Diagnostic queries:**
- "Why is LT-5100 flagged?"
- "What caused the mass-balance warning at 14:30?"
- "Is it safe to increase load on vessel 3?"

**Predictive queries:**
- "Which sensor will need calibration soonest?"
- "How long do I have before we hit a CRITICAL confidence on any sensor?"
- "What is the current risk profile of the plant?"

**Historical queries:**
- "What happened to PT-201 yesterday between 10:00 and 12:00?"
- "How many mass-balance flags were raised in the last 48 hours?"
- "When was FI-101 last calibrated and what was the condition found?"

**Comparison queries:**
- "Compare the current sensor health to 24 hours ago."
- "Which plant in the fleet has the lowest average confidence right now?"

#### Technical Implementation

```python
# Query pipeline
def query_plant(user_query: str, plant_id: str) -> str:
    # Step 1: Retrieve live sensor state
    live_state = get_live_sensor_state(plant_id)
    
    # Step 2: Retrieve relevant history from SQLite
    history = retrieve_relevant_history(user_query, plant_id, window_hours=48)
    
    # Step 3: Retrieve active flags
    flags = get_active_flags(plant_id)
    
    # Step 4: Build grounded context for LLM
    context = build_context(live_state, history, flags)
    
    # Step 5: Claude API call with strict grounding instructions
    response = claude_api.query(
        system=PLANT_QUERY_SYSTEM_PROMPT,
        context=context,
        question=user_query
    )
    
    return response.text
```

**System prompt (abbreviated):**
> You are ConfidenceOS, an industrial plant intelligence assistant. Answer the operator's question using only the sensor data provided. Cite specific sensor IDs, confidence scores, timestamps, and values in your answer. Never extrapolate beyond the data provided. If you cannot answer the question from the available data, say so explicitly and state what additional information would be needed.

#### UI Design

The query panel sits in the right section of the operator dashboard (replacing the sensor health timeline when active — the user can toggle between them). It shows:

- A text input field with placeholder: "Ask anything about this plant..."
- A conversation history showing the last 5 exchanges
- Each answer shows the data sources it drew from as small citation chips below the response
- A "Sources" expandable showing the raw sensor values that grounded the answer

This is the feature that lets a judge who has never seen an HMI walk up and immediately understand what the system knows. It is also the feature that demonstrates the most advanced AI integration in the room.

### 4.4 Module 10 — Incident Forensics & Replay Engine

**Owner:** Backend + frontend
**Language:** Python + React + SQLite
**Estimated effort:** 4 days
**Dependencies:** SQLite sensor history (V1), confidence scoring engine

#### What It Does

The single most powerful demo feature in V2. The Incident Forensics Engine allows any historical time window to be replayed at any speed, showing exactly what every sensor read, what the confidence score was, and what flags were active at every moment in time.

For the demo, the Texas City incident timeline is pre-loaded as a historical dataset. Judges can scrub through the morning of March 23, 2005 — compressed to a few minutes — and watch:

- The sensor reading staying flat at 7.9 feet while the mass-balance line diverges
- The confidence score dropping from 64% to 48% to 12% as the calibration model progresses
- The exact moment ConfidenceOS would have flagged the Physical Inconsistency
- The exact moment the traditional HMI would have showed: nothing

This is not a video. It is a live, interactive replay where the judge controls the playback speed and can pause at any moment to inspect any sensor.

#### Technical Implementation

```
Data Model:
  sensor_reading_log (timestamp, sensor_id, value, confidence_score, flags_active)
  
  → For demo: pre-populate with reconstructed Texas City timeline data
  → For live system: every reading from the V1 simulator is automatically logged

Replay Engine:
  1. User selects time range (or selects "Texas City Incident" preset)
  2. Backend queries sensor_reading_log for that range
  3. Frontend receives the full time-series as a paginated stream
  4. Playback controller renders each second of data at user-selected speed
     (1x, 5x, 10x, 30x, 60x — real time to compressed time)
  5. All V1 dashboard components (confidence bars, mass-balance chart,
     flag panel) render from the historical data instead of live data
  6. Timeline scrubber allows jumping to any point
```

#### Forensics View Additions

Beyond simple replay, the forensics view adds:

**Counterfactual overlay:** A toggle that shows "What ConfidenceOS would have shown" versus "What the original HMI showed." Side-by-side comparison, same sensor, same timestamp. This is the killer visual.

**Incident marker annotations:** Key moments in the Texas City timeline are annotated with CSB report citations: "02:38 — sensor begins misreading," "Shift handover — night shift does not mention fault," "13:04 — tower at 158 feet," "13:20 — explosion."

**Confidence trajectory chart:** A dedicated chart showing every sensor's confidence score over the replayed time window, with tier boundaries drawn as horizontal lines. The judge can watch LT-5100's score fall in real time.

**Export to PDF:** The forensics view can export a PDF showing the full incident reconstruction with sensor data, confidence scores, and flag timeline — formatted as a post-incident analysis report.

### 4.8 Module 14 — Adaptive Threshold Learning

**Owner:** Backend
**Language:** Python + scikit-learn
**Estimated effort:** 2 days
**Dependencies:** SQLite sensor history, confidence scoring engine

#### What It Does

The physical plausibility score in V1 uses static thresholds — fixed upper and lower bounds for what each sensor reading should be. Real plants are not static. Operating conditions shift seasonally, with load changes, and with process modifications. Static thresholds generate false positives during legitimate operating shifts and miss anomalies during abnormal conditions that happen to be within range.

Adaptive Threshold Learning replaces static thresholds with dynamically learned operating envelopes. Every 24 hours, the engine re-fits a Gaussian envelope model to the most recent 72 hours of sensor data (excluding known anomaly periods) and updates the plausibility bounds accordingly.

```
plausibility_score = gaussian_envelope.score(current_reading)

# Reading within 1σ of learned mean → 1.0
# Reading within 2σ → 0.7
# Reading within 3σ → 0.3
# Reading beyond 3σ → 0.0 (physically implausible)
```

The learned envelope is displayed on the sensor health timeline as a shaded band, so engineers can see both the historical readings and the learned normal range in the same view.

A "what-if" mode that lets engineers test failure scenarios without affecting the live view. The engineer selects a sensor, injects a failure mode (from a dropdown), sets the duration and severity, and watches how the confidence engine, mass-balance engine, and predictive failure engine respond — without any impact on the operator's live dashboard.

This is an engineering training and validation tool, and it is the feature that makes ConfidenceOS useful for onboarding new operators: they can experience a realistic sensor failure and watch all of ConfidenceOS's responses in a safe, sandboxed environment.

### 6.2 Data Flow Additions

**New in V2: Persistent Read-Through Logging**
Every sensor reading that passes through the V1 confidence engine is now simultaneously written to `sensor_readings` in SQLite. This is what enables forensics replay and compliance reporting. V1 stored calibration records and anomaly logs. V2 stores every single reading. Estimated storage: ~50KB/hour per plant for 6 sensors at 1 Hz.

**New in V2: Multi-Plant Routing**
The FastAPI backend now accepts a `plant_id` parameter on every endpoint. Three simulator instances run in parallel (ports 8001, 8002, 8003). The frontend routes to the correct instance based on which plant is being viewed. The fleet overview aggregates data from all three instances.

## 8. ABB Integration Strategy

This section is written for the judges, who are ABB employees. It demonstrates that we have thought about how ConfidenceOS would actually deploy in ABB's product ecosystem — not just as a hackathon prototype, but as a real product.

### 8.1 ABB 800xA Integration Path

ABB's flagship DCS platform is System 800xA. ConfidenceOS's intelligence layer (confidence engine, mass-balance engine, predictive engine) is completely decoupled from its data source. The only interface is the sensor data contract: a JSON stream of sensor readings with metadata.

System 800xA exposes sensor data via OPC-UA. ConfidenceOS requires one adapter: an OPC-UA client that reads from 800xA and publishes to the FastAPI WebSocket in the existing schema. This adapter is approximately 150 lines of Python using the `opcua-asyncio` library and represents a 2-day engineering effort.

After that adapter is written, ConfidenceOS runs against real plant data with zero changes to the intelligence layer.

### 8.2 ABB Ability Platform Integration

ABB Ability is ABB's cloud platform for industrial IoT. ConfidenceOS could deploy as an ABB Ability application, with:

- Sensor data ingested from ABB Ability's data lake rather than a local simulator
- Compliance reports stored in ABB Ability's document management system
- Fleet view scaled to real customer fleets (not just 3 simulated plants)
- Predictive failure forecasts fed back into ABB's maintenance management workflow

## 11. Risks and Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Predictive model fits poorly on short history | Medium | Medium | Fall back to linear interpolation when R² < 0.7. Show "insufficient data" label rather than wrong prediction. |
| NL query produces incorrect plant data | Medium | High | Strict grounding prompt. Every answer must cite sensor ID and value. Test with 20 queries before demo. Fallback: show "I cannot answer this from available data." |
| Three simulator instances cause performance degradation | Low | High | Run on separate ports. Lightweight instances — each uses < 50MB RAM. Test on demo machine specifically. |
| D3.js causal graph layout is unstable or ugly | Medium | Low | Use vis.js as fallback — simpler API, more stable layout. Causal graph is not a demo-critical screen. |
| Compliance PDF generates slowly | Low | Medium | Generate asynchronously, show progress. Pre-generate one report before demo as a cached fallback. |
| Forensics replay is jerky or slow | Medium | High | Pre-compute the Texas City scenario into a JSON snapshot. Replay from JSON, not live SQLite queries. |
| Role switching breaks existing V1 features | Medium | High | Role switching is purely a frontend concern — same API, different component rendering. Implement in Zustand as a global role state. Test each role against each screen. |
| Judges cannot use it without help | High | Critical | Prepare quick-start card. Remove all jargon from UI. Test with one team member who did not build it — if they need help, the UI is wrong. |

## Appendix A — KPI Definitions

ConfidenceOS introduces new KPIs for plant health benchmarking that do not exist in traditional HMI products.

**Plant Health Score (PHS)**
Average confidence score across all sensors at a point in time. Range 0–100%. Composite metric visible in fleet view and operator dashboard header.

**Mean Time Between Confidence Failures (MTBCF)**
Average time in hours between events where any sensor crosses into LOW or CRITICAL confidence tier. Higher is better. Analogous to MTBF in reliability engineering but applied to sensor data integrity rather than hardware failure.

**Mass-Balance Integrity Score (MBIS)**
Percentage of time in a given period where all vessels' implied levels (from flow integration) agreed with sensor readings within tolerance. 100% = perfect physical consistency throughout the period.

**Shift Handover Completeness Index (SHCI)**
Percentage of shift changes where a ConfidenceOS handover brief was generated and acknowledged by the incoming operator before they took control. Target: 100%.

**Predictive Lead Time (PLT)**
Average number of hours between when the predictive engine first flagged a sensor as trajectory-to-CRITICAL and when that sensor actually reached CRITICAL. Higher PLT means more warning time. Target: > 4 hours for all sensors.
